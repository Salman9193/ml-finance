D-Mart wins on headline current ratio, but context matters: Titan's inventory is gold (globally liquid commodity), while D-Mart's inventory is perishable/consumer goods (less liquid in a stress scenario).

### Quick Ratio — The Interesting Story

| Company | FY25 | FY24 | What Happened |
|---------|------|------|---------------|
| Titan | very low | low | Gold dominates current assets; Quick Ratio is structurally distorted |
| D-Mart | 0.61 | 1.15 | Sharp decline — ST financial assets redeployed into capex |

For Titan, Quick Ratio is not the right metric because gold is both inventory AND a highly liquid commodity. For D-Mart, the Quick Ratio decline is an important watch signal despite the company's cash-generative model.

### ML Implication
```python
# Sector-adjusted liquidity scoring
# Don't compare raw Quick Ratios across jewellery and grocery retail
# Build sector-specific normalization

def adjusted_quick_ratio(ca, inventory, cl, sector):
    if sector == 'jewellery':
        # Gold inventory is ~80% liquid; apply partial credit
        liquid_inventory = inventory * 0.80
        return (ca - inventory + liquid_inventory) / cl
    elif sector == 'grocery_retail':
        # Standard computation — inventory has low distress liquidation value
        return (ca - inventory) / cl
    else:
        return (ca - inventory) / cl
```

## 9. DuPont Comparison

| DuPont Driver | Titan FY25 | D-Mart FY25 | Who Wins | How |
|--------------|-----------|------------|----------|-----|
| Net Profit Margin | ~6.0% | 5.0% | Titan | Brand premium → higher per-rupee profit |
| Asset Turnover | ~1.0x | **2.44x** | D-Mart | Volume velocity → more revenue per rupee of assets |
| Equity Multiplier | ~2.4x | 1.14x | Titan | Leverage amplifies ROE (two-edged sword) |
| **ROE (product)** | **~14–20%** | **12.6–13.5%** | Roughly equal | Different routes to similar destinations |

### The DuPont Story in One Paragraph

Titan's ROE is built on margin and moderate leverage. D-Mart's ROE is built on pure operational velocity with no leverage whatsoever. If you stripped Titan's leverage (set equity multiplier to 1.14x like D-Mart), its ROE would fall meaningfully. If you gave D-Mart some leverage, its ROE would rise — but management has chosen not to, preserving financial strength. The two companies represent the two poles of the DuPont triangle: **margin-led** (Titan) vs **turnover-led** (D-Mart).

```python
# Comparative DuPont
companies = {
    'Titan': {'npm': 0.060, 'at': 1.01, 'em': 2.45},
    'DMart': {'npm': 0.050, 'at': 2.44, 'em': 1.14},
}

for name, v in companies.items():
    roe = v['npm'] * v['at'] * v['em']
    print(f"{name}: NPM={v['npm']:.1%} × AT={v['at']:.2f}x × EM={v['em']:.2f}x = ROE {roe:.1%}")

# Titan: NPM=6.0% × AT=1.01x × EM=2.45x = ROE ~14.9%
# DMart: NPM=5.0% × AT=2.44x × EM=1.14x = ROE ~13.9%
```

## Final Thought — Two Roads to ROCE of 21%

Both companies generate approximately 21% ROCE. But the journey looks nothing alike:

```
Titan's Path:
High margin (9.6%) × Moderate velocity (1.0x) × Some leverage (2.4x)
= ROCE driven by brand premium + financial amplification

D-Mart's Path:
Low margin (7.8%) × High velocity (2.44x) × Zero leverage (1.14x)
= ROCE driven entirely by operational excellence
```

In ML terms: **same label, completely different features**. This is why sector-naive models fail — a model trained to predict "high ROCE" companies would not recognize that Titan and D-Mart achieve the same output through opposite input vectors. Feature engineering must be sector-aware, and DuPont decomposition is the essential tool for attribution.
